<Callout type="success" title="The good news">

- Hangul is highly regular — far more consistent than English spelling, even though pronunciation still changes predictably by position
- You can learn to *read* all syllables within a few days; fluency comes from practising sounds, not memorising spelling rules
- Around half the consonants are direct matches to sounds you already make in English
- Every syllable is a neat block: (consonant) + vowel + (optional final consonant) — no hidden complexity

</Callout>

## Your 5 biggest challenges

English contrasts two consonant categories: voiced (b, d, g) and voiceless (p, t, k). Korean has
**three** — and two of its vowels simply don't exist in English.

<div className="space-y-3">

<SectionCard variant="danger" title="The three-way stop distinction" badge="Hard">

English only contrasts voiced vs voiceless. Korean has three types for each stop position — lax,
aspirated, and tense. Mixing them up changes the meaning of words entirely.

    <div className="korean-text font-semibold" style={{ color: 'var(--c-2)' }}>불</div>
    <div className="text-xs" style={{ color: 'var(--c-4)' }}>fire</div>
    <div className="text-xs leading-tight" style={{ color: 'var(--c-3)' }}>No air burst — between English b and p</div>
  </div>

The same three-way pattern applies to ㄱ / ㅋ / ㄲ, ㄷ / ㅌ / ㄸ, and ㅈ / ㅊ / ㅉ.

</SectionCard>

<SectionCard variant="warning" title="ㄹ — the Korean flap" badge="Medium">

At syllable **start**, ㄹ is neither "r" nor "l". It's an alveolar flap — the tongue tip taps
the ridge behind your teeth once and bounces off immediately. In American English the "tt" in
*"butter"* or *"water"* is flapped the same way. Don't roll it; don't drawl it.

At syllable **end**, it's a clear lateral "l" like "tall".

<SectionCard variant="danger" title="ㅡ — no English equivalent" badge="Hard">

This unrounded central vowel doesn't exist in English. Try it: say "ee" (ㅣ), keep your lips
in that spread-wide position, then relax your tongue to a flat, neutral position. Or say "hmm"
with your lips spread slightly open rather than pursed. It sounds a bit like a grunt.

</SectionCard>

<SectionCard variant="warning" title="ㅓ vs ㅏ — two open vowels" badge="Medium">

ㅏ is the "a" in "father" — jaw drops, tongue flat and low. ㅓ is harder to place: the closest
English approximation is the "u" in "cup" or "uh" in "but" — an open, unrounded vowel. It is
*not* "eo" as in "leopard" — that romanization misleads.

<SectionCard variant="success" title="ㅇ — context-dependent" badge="Easy once you know the rule">

At syllable **start**, ㅇ is completely silent — every syllable block must start with a consonant
visually, so ㅇ is the placeholder when there's no actual initial consonant. At syllable **end**,
it's "ng" as in "sing". Perfectly consistent once you know it.

## Reading in blocks, not letters

Korean writes syllables as visual blocks, not a linear letter string. Train yourself to read each
block as a single unit of sound. The word 한글 is two blocks: 한 (han) and 글 (geul).

Vowels that hang to the right of the initial consonant are vertical (ㅏ ㅓ ㅣ…); vowels that sit
below are horizontal (ㅗ ㅜ ㅡ…). The final consonant, when present, sits at the bottom of the block.

## Why romanization will slow you down

<Callout type="warning" title="⚠ Romanization is a scaffold — remove it as soon as possible">

- **"eu" for ㅡ** doesn't sound like any English "eu". Your brain will hear the wrong sound.
- **"eo" for ㅓ** is not the "eo" in "leopard" or "people".
- **ㄱ** can appear as "g" or "k" in romanization depending on position — the spelling shifts, but the letter itself does not.

Use audio as your phonetic reference. Hangul was designed in 1443 to precisely represent sounds — trust the script itself.

</Callout>
